# Jin et al. 2017 - Vi-TCV Vaccine Trial: Anti-Vi IgG Waning Extraction

## Citation
Jin C, Gibani MM, Moore M, Juel HB, Jones E, Meiring J, Harris V, Gardner J, Nebykova A, Kerridge SA, Hill J, Thomaides-Brears H, Blohmke CJ, Yu L-M, Angus B, Pollard AJ. Efficacy and immunogenicity of a Vi-tetanus toxoid conjugate vaccine in the prevention of typhoid fever using a controlled human infection model of *Salmonella* Typhi: a randomised controlled, phase 2b trial. *Lancet*. 2017;390:2472-2480. doi:10.1016/S0140-6736(17)32149-9

**ClinicalTrials.gov:** NCT02324751

---

## Study Overview

- **Primary question (for this extraction)**: What do vaccine-induced anti-Vi IgG kinetics and vaccine efficacy data tell us about Vi-mediated immunity and its durability?
- **Study type**: Randomised controlled phase 2b trial using a controlled human infection model (CHIM)
- **Institution**: Oxford Vaccine Group, Department of Paediatrics, University of Oxford; Centre for Clinical Vaccinology and Tropical Medicine, Churchill Hospital, Oxford, UK
- **Population**: Healthy typhoid-naive adults aged 18-60 years, no prior typhoid vaccination, no residence in endemic country >=6 months
- **Enrollment period**: August 18, 2015 - November 4, 2016
- **Funding**: Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation; European Commission FP7 (ADITEC)

**[CRITICAL]** This paper was designed as a vaccine efficacy trial, not a waning study. Anti-Vi IgG was measured at only two time points (pre-vaccination and day 28 post-vaccination = time of challenge). There is **no longitudinal post-challenge anti-Vi IgG follow-up** reported. The paper's value for the waning metastudy comes from: (a) the magnitude of vaccine-induced Vi boosting vs. natural-infection Vi boosting, (b) the correlate of protection analysis, and (c) the implications for why Aiemjoy's "Vi decay rate = 0" is a signal-to-noise artifact.

| **Day 28 post-vaccination** | 8.0 (5.2-12.2) | 562.9 (396.9-798.8) | 140.5 (91.0-216.9) | p.2477, Supp Table S2 |

*No significant differences in pre-vaccination titres between groups (Control vs Vi-TT: p=0.27; Control vs Vi-PS: p=0.64; Vi-TT vs Vi-PS: p=0.50). All day 28 pairwise comparisons p<0.0001.* (Supp Table S2)

### Fold-Rise from Vaccination

| Group | Pre-vaccination GMT | Day 28 GMT | Fold-rise (GMT ratio) | Seroconversion rate |
|-------|--------------------|-----------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **Control** | 7.7 | 8.0 | ~1.0x | N/A |
| **Vi-TT** | 5.6 | 562.9 | **~100x** | **100%** |
| **Vi-PS** | 5.6 | 140.5 | **~22x** (from 6.3 baseline) | **88.6%** |

*Seroconversion rates from p.2477. Fold-rise calculated from reported GMTs.*

**[CRITICAL]** Vi-TT vaccination produces a ~100-fold rise in anti-Vi IgG within 28 days. Vi-PS produces a ~22-fold rise. Compare this to natural infection in the Aiemjoy 2022 data: the 28-day case-to-population-mean boosting ratio for Vi IgG was only **1.5x (IQR 1.0-2.1)** (Aiemjoy p.e583). Vaccine-induced Vi boosting exceeds natural-infection Vi boosting by roughly **two orders of magnitude for Vi-TT** and **one order of magnitude for Vi-PS**.

| Control | 13/34 | 38% |
| Vi-TT | 12/41 | 29% |

## Anti-Vi IgG Subclass Responses (Day 28, Vaccine Groups Only)

### Overall Subclass GMTs (EU/mL)

| Subclass | Vi-TT GMT (95% CI) | Vi-PS GMT (95% CI) | p-value (Vi-TT vs Vi-PS) | Source |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|--------|
| **IgG1** | 85.6 (61.5, 119.2) | 14.0 (9.2, 21.3) | <0.0001 | Supp Table S2 |
| **IgG2** | 49.0 (33.8, 71.1) | 15.1 (9.8, 23.2) | 0.0001 | Supp Table S2 |
| **IgG3** | 116.6 (84.5, 161.0) | 27.6 (18.9, 40.4) | <0.0001 | Supp Table S2 |

**[NOTE]** Vi-TT induced significantly higher titres of all three IgG subclasses (IgG1, IgG2, IgG3) compared to Vi-PS (all p<=0.0001). Vi-PS, as a T-cell-independent polysaccharide antigen, is known to preferentially induce IgG2 (ref 26 in paper: Vidarsson 2014). The conjugate vaccine (Vi-TT) shifts the response toward IgG1 and IgG3, which are more functional for opsonization and complement activation. This subclass difference may partly explain why Vi-TT showed higher VE than Vi-PS despite both targeting Vi.

## Correlate of Protection Analysis

### Anti-Vi IgG at Time of Challenge vs. Diagnosis Outcome

| Group | Diagnosed GMT (EU/mL, 95% CI) | Undiagnosed GMT (EU/mL, 95% CI) | p-value (D vs U) | Source |
|-------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|------------------|--------|
| **Control** | 7.1 (4.4, 11.4) | 12.0 (3.8, 37.9) | 0.19 | Supp Table S2, Fig 3A |
| **Vi-TT** | 522 (241, 1129) | 586 (396, 868) | 0.84 | Supp Table S2, Fig 3A |
| **Vi-PS** | 73 (43, 122) | 207 (116, 372) | 0.0070 | Supp Table S2, Fig 3A |

**[CRITICAL]** The pattern of correlate significance differs strikingly between vaccine types:

- **Vi-PS**: Strong dose-response within the vaccine group. Diagnosed individuals had significantly lower day 28 anti-Vi IgG (73 EU/mL) than undiagnosed (207 EU/mL), p=0.0070. Anti-Vi IgG quantity is a clear correlate of protection for Vi-PS.
- **Vi-TT**: No significant difference (522 vs. 586, p=0.84). Vi-TT induced uniformly high titres in nearly all recipients, creating a "ceiling effect" that obscures the dose-response. The authors interpret this as suggesting that **antibody functionality (not just quantity) matters for Vi-TT protection** (p.2479).
- **Control**: No difference, as expected (these participants had only background Vi levels).

### Logistic Regression: Anti-Vi IgG Titre and Probability of Diagnosis

| Model | Odds Ratio (95% CI) | p-value | Source |
|-------|--------------------|---------|----|
| **Adjusted for vaccine group** | 0.37 (0.15-0.88) | <0.0001 | p.2477 |
| **Unadjusted** | 0.35 (0.21-0.59) | 0.0001 | p.2477 |

*OR is per log10 increase in anti-Vi IgG titre at day of challenge.*

**[NOTE]** The logistic regression curve (Supp Figure S3) shows a continuous inverse relationship between anti-Vi IgG titre and probability of typhoid diagnosis. Individual data points from all three groups fall on a single curve, suggesting the relationship is titre-dependent regardless of how the titre was achieved (vaccine or background). The authors state: "An absolute threshold of protection, above which individuals are 100% protected from disease, could not be identified from these data" (p.2479).

**[ASSISTANT-PROPOSED]** Reading Figure S3 by eye: at anti-Vi IgG ~10 EU/mL (typical control/baseline), probability of diagnosis is ~0.65-0.75. At ~100 EU/mL (typical Vi-PS), probability drops to ~0.35-0.45. At ~500-1000 EU/mL (typical Vi-TT), probability drops to ~0.15-0.25. The curve does not reach zero even at the highest titres (~2000+ EU/mL), consistent with the observation that some Vi-TT recipients were still diagnosed despite very high anti-Vi IgG. These visual readings should be treated as approximate. `[NEEDS RE-EXTRACTION]` for precise values from digitized figure data.

### VE Estimates by Endpoint Definition

| Endpoint | Control AR | Vi-TT AR | Vi-TT VE (95% CI) | Vi-PS AR | Vi-PS VE (95% CI) | Source |
|----------|-----------|---------|-------------------|---------|-------------------|--------|
| **Composite (primary)** | 24/31 (77%) | 13/37 (35%) | 54.6% (26.8-71.8) | 13/35 (37%) | 52.0% (23.2-70.0) | Table 2, p.2476 |
| **Fever >=38.0C + bacteraemia** | 16/31 (52%) | 4/37 (11%) | 79.1% (43.8-92.2) | 8/35 (23%) | 55.7% (11.0-78.0) | Supp Table S1 |
| **Fever >=38.0C with subsequent bacteraemia** | 13/31 (42%) | 2/37 (5%) | **87.1% (47.2-96.9)** | 7/35 (20%) | 52.3% (-4.2-78.2) | Supp Table S1 |

**[CRITICAL]** This is a single-time-point challenge at ~28 days post-vaccination. The study provides a **snapshot of VE at one month**, not a VE waning curve. There is no long-term follow-up to assess VE decline over years. The study **cannot directly address whether VE wanes**.

**[NOTE]** However, the authors note that the CHIM VE estimate (54.6% for Vi-TT using composite endpoint) is **lower than field trial VE estimates** for similar Vi-TCV vaccines. They state: "the protective effect of Vi-TT in endemic settings is higher" (p.2478) and note that Vi-PS efficacy in this CHIM (52%) is "notably less than that observed in field trials of Vi-PS, where efficacy is estimated at 69% within the first year following vaccination" (p.2478, referencing Anwar et al. 2014). They attribute this discrepancy to: (1) the high challenge dose overwhelming vaccine-induced immunity, and (2) differences between healthy typhoid-naive adults and endemic populations with prior exposure.

## Evidence Bearing on Vi IgG Waning (Indirect)

### What This Study Can Tell Us

This study was not designed to measure antibody waning, but several findings provide indirect constraints on Vi IgG waning rates.

#### 1. Magnitude of Vaccine-Induced Vi Boosting

| Source of Vi boost | Anti-Vi IgG level | Fold above baseline | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vi-TT vaccination (day 28)** | 562.9 EU/mL | ~100x above 5.6 EU/mL baseline | p.2477, Supp Table S2 |
| **Vi-PS vaccination (day 28)** | 140.5 EU/mL | ~22x above 6.3 EU/mL baseline | p.2477, Supp Table S2 |
| **Natural infection, Aiemjoy overall** | y1 = 542.7 EU, y0 = 126.7 EU | ~4.3x (peak/baseline) | Aiemjoy Table S1 |
| **Natural infection, 28d boost ratio** | 1.5x population mean | 1.5x | Aiemjoy p.e583 |

**[CRITICAL]** The comparison is striking. Vi-TT produces Vi IgG levels (~563 EU/mL) comparable to the peak Vi IgG after natural infection in the Aiemjoy model (y1 = 542.7 EU), but from a **much lower baseline** (5.6 vs. 126.7 EU). The fold-rise from Vi-TT vaccination (~100x) dwarfs the fold-rise from natural infection (~1.5x above population mean, or ~4.3x peak-over-baseline in the model). This means that if both vaccine-induced and infection-induced Vi IgG wane at the same intrinsic rate, waning should be **far more detectable** in vaccine recipients because the signal-to-noise ratio is ~100x vs. ~1.5x.

#### 2. External Evidence of Vi Antibody Persistence Post-Vaccination

The paper cites several references about Vi antibody durability that provide context (p.2479):

| Finding | Reference | What it tells us |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Immunogenicity in children from 2 months of age | Ref 12: Vu Dinh 2011 | Vi-TCV immunogenic in infants |
| Antibody persistence up to 8 years post-vaccination | Ref 29: Lanh 2003 (NEJM 349:1390-91) | **Vi-rEPA conjugate vaccine: persistent efficacy of Vi conjugate vaccine against typhoid fever in young children** |
| Detection of higher avidity Vi-antibody | Ref 24: *not extracted* | Higher quality antibody 2 years after priming |
| Boosting of antibody titres with re-vaccination 2 years after priming | Ref 14: Mohan 2015 | TCV booster responses detectable |

**[CRITICAL]** The Lanh et al. 2003 reference (NEJM) is particularly important: it reports **persistent efficacy of Vi-rEPA conjugate vaccine against typhoid fever in young children** (2-5 year olds, Vietnamese field trial, estimated ~89% efficacy, ref 15: Lin et al. 2001 NEJM 344:1263-9). The fact that a different Vi conjugate vaccine maintained protection for multiple years implies that Vi-mediated immunity has meaningful duration, but the paper does not report whether anti-Vi IgG levels declined over that follow-up period. `[NEEDS RE-EXTRACTION]` -- the Lanh 2003 and Lin 2001 papers should be read directly for anti-Vi IgG kinetics data.

#### 3. The "Overwhelmed Immunity" Observation

The authors note (p.2478-2479):

> "In view of this high attack rate, it is plausible that vaccine-induced immunity in some Vi-vaccinees was overwhelmed by the large bacterial inoculum resulting in infection, despite the presence of Vi antibody."

And:

> "*S* Typhi is primarily an intracellular bacterium that highly regulates Vi-capsule expression" (p.2479, refs 27, 28)

**[ASSISTANT-PROPOSED]** This is directly relevant to the waning question. Some Vi-TT recipients with **very high** anti-Vi IgG (the top of the Vi-TT distribution in Figure S3, ~1000-2000+ EU/mL) still became infected. This suggests that:
1. Anti-Vi IgG quantity alone does not determine protection -- functional quality, innate immunity, and bacterial evasion (Vi capsule downregulation during intracellular phase) all contribute.
2. Even at peak vaccine-induced levels (~560 EU/mL GMT, with individual values ranging higher), protection is not absolute against a high challenge dose.
3. Therefore, the Vi IgG level required for protection against natural (lower-dose) exposure may be substantially lower than what's needed in the CHIM. This means that even if Vi IgG wanes significantly from its vaccine-induced peak, residual levels could still provide protection against typical endemic exposures for extended periods.

## What This Study Cannot Tell Us About Waning

**[CRITICAL]** The following gaps limit the study's direct contribution to Vi waning characterization:

1. **No post-challenge anti-Vi IgG measurements.** Serum samples were collected at screening, pre-vaccination, and day 28 post-vaccination only. There is no measurement of anti-Vi IgG at 3 months, 6 months, 1 year, or 2+ years post-vaccination. The study cannot provide a Vi IgG waning curve.

2. **No long-term VE follow-up.** The CHIM provides VE at one time point (~28 days post-vaccination). There is no re-challenge at later time points to assess whether VE declines.

3. **Single challenge dose.** All participants received the same (high) challenge dose. There is no data on VE against lower, more realistic exposure doses where waning Vi levels might be more consequential.

4. **Non-endemic population.** All participants were typhoid-naive UK adults. Their Vi IgG waning kinetics may differ from endemic populations who have pre-existing immune memory from prior exposures.

## Synthesis: How Jin 2017 Informs the Aiemjoy Censoring Argument

**[CRITICAL]** This section draws on both Jin 2017 and Aiemjoy 2022 to build the case that Aiemjoy's Vi IgG "decay rate = 0" is a detection artifact, and that vaccine data are needed to characterize true Vi waning.

### The Signal-to-Noise Argument, Quantified

| Parameter | Vi-TT vaccine (Jin) | Natural infection (Aiemjoy) | Ratio (vaccine/natural) |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Absolute anti-Vi IgG at ~28 days** | 562.9 EU/mL | y1 = 542.7 EU (model peak) | ~1.0x |
| **Baseline anti-Vi IgG** | 5.6 EU/mL (naive UK adults) | y0 = 126.7 EU (endemic adults, overall) | 0.044x |
| **Fold-rise above baseline** | ~100x | ~4.3x (y1/y0 overall); 1.5x (28d case/pop mean) | **23x to 67x** |
| **Signal available for waning detection** | 557 EU/mL above baseline | ~416 EU (y1-y0 overall); much less in practice | N/A |
| **Baseline as fraction of peak** | 1% | 23% (overall); 31% (age 5-15); 31% (age 16+) | N/A |

The critical insight: **Vi-TT vaccination and natural infection produce similar peak Vi IgG levels** (~540-560 EU), but the vaccine operates from a ~20x lower baseline. In the Aiemjoy endemic cohort, Vi IgG only needs to decline by ~4-fold from peak before it becomes indistinguishable from the high endemic baseline. In a vaccine cohort, Vi IgG can decline by ~100-fold before reaching the (low naive) baseline. If Vi IgG has a half-life of, say, 1-2 years, a vaccine cohort would show clear decline over a 2-year follow-up, while an endemic infection cohort would not -- the signal would be swallowed by the baseline long before the observation window closes.

### The Missing Experiment

**[ASSISTANT-PROPOSED]** The experiment that would definitively resolve the Vi waning question is:
- Take Vi-TT vaccinees (starting from ~560 EU/mL with ~5 EU/mL baseline)
- Measure anti-Vi IgG serially at 3, 6, 12, 24, 36, 48 months post-vaccination
- Fit a decay model to the resulting trajectories

With a ~100x fold-rise and minimal baseline noise, even slow waning (half-life >2 years) would produce a clearly detectable decline over the observation window. This experiment is essentially what the TyVAC field trials (mentioned in the paper as ongoing, p.2479-2480) and subsequent Typbar-TCV immunogenicity studies should provide.

**[NOTE]** Several subsequent studies have been published since Jin 2017 that may provide exactly this data:
- Mohan et al. 2015 (ref 14 in paper) reports Typbar-TCV immunogenicity in endemic settings with multi-year follow-up
- The TyVAC trials in Nepal, Bangladesh, and Malawi include immunogenicity arms with longitudinal sampling
- Shakya et al. 2019 (Lancet, TyVAC Nepal) and subsequent papers report multi-year efficacy and immunogenicity follow-up

These should be prioritized for extraction in the waning metastudy. `[NEEDS RE-EXTRACTION]` -- TyVAC immunogenicity papers.

### Reconciling Vaccine Protection with "No Natural Vi Waning"

The Jin 2017 data create a coherent but nuanced picture:

1. **Vi-mediated immunity exists.** Anti-Vi IgG is a continuous correlate of protection (OR 0.37 per log10 increase, p<0.0001). This is demonstrated across the full range from control (~8 EU/mL) to Vi-TT (~563 EU/mL). Higher Vi IgG = lower probability of typhoid. (p.2477, Fig S3)

2. **Vi-based vaccines work because they produce ENORMOUS Vi boosting.** Vi-TT produces ~100x fold-rise; Vi-PS ~22x fold-rise. These levels push recipients high enough on the dose-response curve (Fig S3) to achieve ~55-87% efficacy depending on the endpoint definition. (p.2476-2477, Supp Table S1)

3. **Natural infection barely moves the Vi needle.** In endemic populations, natural typhoid infection produces only a 1.5x boost in Vi IgG above the population mean (Aiemjoy p.e583). In the Aiemjoy model, the peak-to-baseline ratio (y1/y0) is ~4.3x overall but only ~2.4x in the 5-15 age group where most data exist.

4. **If Vi wanes (as it presumably does -- all antibody responses eventually wane), the natural-infection Vi signal is lost in noise before waning becomes detectable.** The Aiemjoy model's "decay rate = 0" for Vi IgG is a consequence of the model structure right-censoring the decay parameter when signal-to-noise is poor. The vaccine context proves that Vi IgG CAN be far above baseline -- the question is whether natural infection ever pushes it far enough above baseline for decay to be measurable.

5. **The control group in Jin 2017 is the Rosetta Stone.** Unvaccinated UK adults with no typhoid exposure have a Vi IgG GMT of ~8 EU/mL (with 38% having detectable levels >7.4 EU/mL). In endemic Nepalese adults (Aiemjoy), baseline Vi IgG is ~202 EU/mL. This 25-fold difference in baseline is entirely due to cumulative endemic exposure. If Vi truly never waned, endemic adults should accumulate Vi from every exposure, and the baseline should grow dramatically with age. Instead, cross-sectional Vi IgG shows **no age trend** (Aiemjoy p.e583; Pulickal et al. 2009). This is consistent with Vi waning at a rate that approximately balances the rate of new boosting exposures.

### Implications for Modeling

**[ASSISTANT-PROPOSED, MIKE CONFIRMED]** The combined Jin + Aiemjoy picture suggests the following modeling framework for Vi-mediated immunity:

1. **Vi IgG wanes**, but the waning rate is poorly constrained by natural-infection data. The Aiemjoy "decay rate = 0" should be treated as a **non-informative upper bound**, not a parameter estimate.

2. **Vaccine immunogenicity data should be used to constrain Vi waning rates.** Vi-TT and Vi-PS produce known, large Vi boosts from low baselines. Longitudinal post-vaccination anti-Vi IgG data (from TyVAC and other trials) can provide clean waning rate estimates that are not confounded by high endemic baselines.

3. **The correlate of protection curve from Jin Figure S3 provides the bridge** between anti-Vi IgG levels and protection probability. Once a Vi waning rate is established (from vaccine data), one can project the time course of protection by mapping declining Vi IgG levels onto the dose-response curve.

4. **The population-level significance of natural-infection Vi boosting is an open question that requires dynamic modeling.** The 1.5× per-infection boost is small, but its cumulative effect depends on the balance between exposure rate (which varies dramatically across settings) and waning rate (which we are trying to constrain). Different endemic settings have different Vi baseline distributions and age-structured exposure profiles. Whether repeated small boosts accumulate to meaningful population immunity cannot be determined from the per-infection signal-to-noise ratio alone — it requires integrating the waning rate, exposure rate, and correlate-of-protection curve through a dynamic transmission model.

## Fit Role Assessment

**[ASSISTANT-PROPOSED]** **SUPPORTING** for waning characterization, **CORE** for interpreting the Aiemjoy Vi censoring result.

This paper does not provide direct Vi IgG waning data (only two time points, no post-vaccination decline measured). However, it is indispensable for the waning metastudy because:

1. It quantifies the **magnitude gap** between vaccine-induced and natural-infection Vi boosting (~100x vs. 1.5x fold-rise), which is the central explanation for why Aiemjoy cannot detect Vi waning.
2. It establishes anti-Vi IgG as a **continuous correlate of protection** with a quantitative dose-response relationship (Fig S3, logistic regression OR), enabling projection of how antibody waning translates to VE waning.
3. It provides the **naive population baseline** (5.6-7.7 EU/mL) against which vaccine-induced waning can be measured, free from endemic confounding.
4. It identifies the TyVAC trials and other longitudinal Vi-TCV immunogenicity studies as the **highest-priority sources** for direct Vi waning rate estimation.
